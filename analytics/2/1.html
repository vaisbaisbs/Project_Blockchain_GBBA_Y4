What is an Active Address ?

An active address refers to a unique blockchain address that has participated in at least one transaction (sending or receiving) over a given period. It focuses on real-time activity. This metric is widely used in on-chain analysis as a proxy for measuring the level of user activity and engagement within a network. Unlike price data, which can be heavily influenced by speculation and market sentiment, active addresses provide insight into the actual usage of a blockchain. In that way, they are considered a key indicator of a blockchain’s fundamental health. For a more detailed explanation of this metric, you can read this article: What Are Active Addresses in Cryptocurrency and Why Are They Important?.

Distribution of active addresses within the Portfolio

This graph illustrates a highly heterogeneous distribution of active addresses across the cryptocurrencies included in the portfolio. It clearly shows that TRON dominates significantly, with more than 3.6 million active addresses, suggesting a solid user base. It is followed by Ethereum (~953k) and Bitcoin (~587k), which exhibit relatively high levels of activity, although still substantially lower than TRON. Litecoin also stands out with approximately 280k active addresses, while the remaining cryptocurrencies display lower activity levels. This result is particularly interesting because it contrasts with the common expectation that Bitcoin and Ethereum dominate across all dimensions, given their leading positions in terms of market capitalization and global recognition. However, caution is required when interpreting TRON’s dominance, as a large share of its activity is driven by low-cost stablecoin transfers which may not necessarily reflect high-value economic activity.

Recommendations

First, it would be advisable to maintain or slightly increase exposure to assets such as Ethereum and Bitcoin, which combine relatively high levels of activity with strong and diversified use cases. These networks benefit not only from user engagement but also from broader ecosystem development, making them more resilient over time.

Second, while TRON’s activity levels are impressive, it would be prudent to approach this asset with caution. The portfolio should not over-rely on it as a core component, given that its activity is largely concentrated in specific use cases (stablecoin transfers). A more measured allocation would help mitigate the risk associated with overestimating its fundamental value.

Finally, assets with low levels of active addresses should be carefully reassessed. If they do not show signs of increasing adoption or improving usage, their weight in the portfolio could be reduced. Alternatively, they could be maintained as speculative positions but with limited exposure. In essence, the key recommendation is to prioritize sustainable and diversified network activity, rather than focusing solely on high activity volumes.

Conclusion

The analysis of active addresses shows that not all cryptocurrencies in the portfolio exhibit the same level of adoption. While some assets benefit from strong network activity, this activity is not always synonymous with sustainable value creation. As a result, the overall health of the portfolio can be considered moderate but uneven, with a degree of reliance on a few dominant networks. To enhance its robustness, the portfolio should gradually shift toward assets that combine high levels of activity with diversified and meaningful use cases.
